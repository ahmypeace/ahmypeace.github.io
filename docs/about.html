Hi! This is me…

I am a Ph.D. student in the Lew Klein College of Media and Communication’s, where I study the relationships among artificial intelligence, communication, institutions, and public life.

My work brings together Human–Machine Communication, computational social science, AI governance, and critical technology studies. I use computational and qualitative methods to investigate how AI systems are imagined, developed, governed, represented, and experienced across different cultural and institutional settings.

At the center of my research is a concern with power. I am interested in who defines the language of responsible AI, whose knowledge becomes embedded in technological systems, how institutions distribute responsibility when those systems cause harm, and how communities that have historically been peripheral to technological development are positioned within global AI futures.

Much of my work pays particular attention to Africa and the Global South. I do not approach these regions merely as sites where technologies are adopted or where familiar theories are applied. I study them as places where people, institutions, and communities actively negotiate technological change, produce knowledge, challenge dominant assumptions, and imagine alternative digital futures.

From Federal Polytechnic Oko to Graduate Study

My academic journey began at Federal Polytechnic Oko, where I earned a Higher National Diploma in Mass Communication. My training introduced me to journalism, broadcasting, public communication, media production, and the processes through which societies interpret change, and the professional routines that determine what becomes visible.

During my master’s degree in Communication Studies at Northern Illinois University, I became increasingly interested in the arrival of artificial intelligence in newsrooms. I wanted to understand AI not only as a collection of tools, but also as a communicative presence that journalists were learning to interpret, trust, question, and incorporate into their work.

My master’s research was advised by Dr. Andrea L. Guzman, whose scholarship helped establish Human–Machine Communication as an important area of communication research.

My thesis, Journalists’ Perception of Artificial Intelligence: The Case of Nigerian Newsrooms, drew on in-depth interviews with reporters, editors, producers, and fact-checkers. The study examined how Nigerian journalists made sense of AI within their everyday professional practices.

The journalists I interviewed described AI as a source of speed, efficiency, and creative assistance, but they also raised concerns about verification, cultural accuracy, professional judgment, newsroom policy, and the limitations of systems developed without adequate knowledge of African contexts. Their experiences showed that the adoption of AI is never simply technical. It is shaped by institutional resources, professional norms, cultural expectations, and unequal technological infrastructures.

That project provided the foundation for my continuing work on AI, journalism, trust, and institutional change. It also strengthened my commitment to conducting research that places African experiences within broader theoretical and policy conversations about emerging technologies.

Doctoral Research at Temple

I am now pursuing my Ph.D. under the guidance of Dr. Andrew Iliadis at Temple University’s Lew Klein College of Media and Communication.

My doctoral work expands beyond the newsroom to examine the broader social, institutional, and infrastructural life of artificial intelligence. I study how technology companies, governments, media organizations, consultants, and international institutions construct ideas about AI safety, responsibility, risk, development, and technological progress.

My research considers both the discourse surrounding AI and the material systems that support it. I am especially interested in the relationships among data infrastructures, institutional authority, corporate governance, platform power, and global inequality.

Across my doctoral projects, I ask questions such as:

  • How do corporations define and allocate responsibility for AI systems?
  • How do governance frameworks change as they move across regions?
  • How are Africa and Latin America constructed within narratives of AI expansion?
  • How do people develop trust in systems that are difficult to understand or evaluate?
  • How can computational methods support critical and culturally grounded communication research?

Current Research

One strand of my work examines how leading AI companies describe responsibility in their official governance, safety, and evaluation documents. Through computational analysis, I study how companies distribute responsibility among models, developers, users, organizational procedures, standards, and institutions.

A second strand investigates the movement of AI governance frameworks across national and regional contexts. This work examines the relationship between European regulatory influence, African data protection laws, national AI strategies, institutional capacity, and local development priorities.

My project on the Global South as a speculative AI market analyzes how technology companies, consultancies, policy organizations, and investment actors construct Africa and Latin America as emerging frontiers for AI deployment. Rather than treating markets as already existing, the study examines how discourse actively produces expectations about future consumers, workers, infrastructures, and opportunities.

I am also conducting computational research on deepfake coverage across African newspapers. This project studies what journalists are talking about when they report on deepfakes, including elections, criminality, sexual and gendered harm, regulation, public awareness, technological innovation, and geopolitical risk.

Alongside my work on AI, I study digital culture more broadly. My research on Nigerian podcasting examines how conversational programs create spaces for parasocial intimacy, pragmatic feminism, digital sisterhood, and the renegotiation of contemporary Nigerian womanhood.

Although these projects address different media and institutional settings, they share a common concern with how communication constructs technological meaning and how those meanings shape power, policy, and public life.

Methods and Research Practice

I work across computational, qualitative, and mixed-methods traditions. My computational research includes natural language processing, computational text analysis, semantic similarity, embeddings, classification, topic modeling, network analysis, entity analysis, statistical comparison, web-based data collection, and data visualization.

I primarily use Python and R, alongside tools and libraries such as Jupyter, pandas, spaCy, scikit-learn, quanteda, ATLAS.ti, Git, and GitHub.

My qualitative training includes in-depth interviewing, thematic analysis, critical discourse analysis, framing analysis, and comparative media research. I approach computational methods as tools for answering substantive social questions rather than as substitutes for theory, interpretation, or contextual knowledge.

This combination allows me to move between large-scale patterns and close attention to language, institutions, professional practice, and lived experience.

Research Beyond Academia

Before beginning my Ph.D., I developed a professional career across journalism, communications, education, corporate work, and technology-adjacent organizational environments.

I worked as a field journalist with the Anambra State Ministry of Transportation, an experience that gave me direct exposure to public institutions, reporting, government communication, and the relationship between policy and everyday life.

I later worked in communications and corporate strategy, eventually progressing into a senior specialist role at KeHE Distributors. In that environment, I worked within a large and complex organization where communication, data, coordination, and operational decisions had to align across teams. The experience strengthened my understanding of how institutional priorities are implemented in practice, and how organizational incentives can differ from formal policies and stated values.

I also worked with the Illinois Tutoring Initiative, a statewide program supporting K–12 learners. That role connected my interests in education and access with direct work in a structured learning environment.

These experiences continue to shape my academic questions. My research on governance and responsibility is informed not only by scholarship, but also by firsthand familiarity with the routines, constraints, and decision-making processes of real organizations.

Where I Am Headed

My long-term goal is to contribute to the development and evaluation of trustworthy, accountable, and socially grounded artificial intelligence.

I am particularly interested in research roles that connect computational analysis with questions of AI risk, trust and safety, information integrity, human–AI interaction, institutional accountability, and responsible technological development.

I hope to build a career that moves across academia, industry, and public policy. I want my research to contribute to theory, but I also want it to inform the design of governance systems, evaluation practices, organizational standards, and technologies that respond more thoughtfully to the diversity of the societies in which they are deployed.

Ultimately, my work is driven by the belief that artificial intelligence is not only a technical system. It is also a communicative, institutional, and political project. Understanding its consequences requires paying attention to the people who build it, the organizations that govern it, the stories through which it is made meaningful, and the communities whose futures are increasingly shaped by it.

Service, Mentorship, and Community

My commitment to education extends beyond my own academic journey. I understand education as more than individual achievement. For me, it is also a means of widening access, sharing knowledge, and helping others imagine possibilities that may initially feel out of reach.

Having experienced how difficult it can be to locate trustworthy information, navigate unfamiliar admissions systems, and gain recognition for an HND, I wanted to make that journey less isolating for others.

I founded HNDScholarsHub, a nonprofit and peer-support initiative that helps Higher National Diploma holders identify and pursue graduate education opportunities around the world. The initiative shares scholarship opportunities, application guidance, admissions resources, and practical advice for students navigating the path from polytechnic education to master’s and doctoral study.

HNDScholarsHub emerged directly from my own experience. I had learned that HND holders could succeed in highly competitive graduate programs, yet accurate information was often fragmented, and many prospective applicants had already been told that their qualifications made further study impossible. Through the initiative, I have worked to challenge that assumption and build a community where HND graduates can learn from one another, ask questions, access reliable information, and pursue their ambitions with greater confidence.

I also founded Back to School with Amy, an education and community initiative dedicated to supporting children and families through the provision of school supplies and other educational resources. The initiative reflects my belief that access to education begins long before the classroom, and that small interventions can create meaningful opportunities for young learners.

These initiatives remain closely connected to my research. Whether I am studying the unequal development of artificial intelligence, helping a student navigate the graduate admissions process, or supporting children returning to school, I am ultimately interested in the systems that shape access to knowledge and opportunity. Across both my scholarship and community work, I ask whose knowledge is recognized, who is given the opportunity to participate, and whose future is imagined as possible.

As I continue developing as a scholar, I hope to mentor emerging researchers, particularly students from African and other historically underrepresented backgrounds who are interested in communication, computational research, and the social implications of artificial intelligence. I believe that scholarship carries a responsibility beyond publication. It should also create pathways, build communities, and expand opportunities for those who follow.
